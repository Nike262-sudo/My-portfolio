DesignMock AI

Designing an AI interview coach that helps Product Designers practice realistic interviews, receive recruiter-style feedback and improve through deliberate practice.

DesignMock AI is a concept product exploring how artificial intelligence can help Product, UX and UI Designers prepare for interviews through realistic mock sessions, structured evaluation and personalised improvement plans.

The Opportunity

Product designers spend months refining portfolios, yet many still struggle to communicate their thinking during interviews.

Designing a strong product and explaining design thinking in an interview are two very different skills. In day-to-day work, designers have time to gather context, explore trade-offs and refine rationale. Interviews compress all of that into a high-pressure conversation where clarity, structure and confidence matter as much as craft.

Most hiring teams are not only evaluating visual output; they are evaluating how decisions were made. They want to hear a coherent story: what problem was framed, what alternatives were considered, what constraints shaped the outcome and what measurable results followed. Without that narrative, good work often appears shallow.

Existing preparation methods often rely on static documents, one-off mock questions or generic tips that fail to recreate real interview pressure. Designers are left practising in isolation, without timely critique or a system that helps them improve over repeated sessions. DesignMock AI was framed to close that gap with realistic practice and recruiter-style feedback loops.

The landing experience introduces DesignMock AI by immediately focusing on realistic interview practice and recruiter-style AI feedback instead of technical features.

Why This Matters

Knowing how to design is different from knowing how to communicate design decisions.

Interview performance depends on storytelling, structured thinking and measurable impact.

Current preparation resources rarely recreate the pressure or depth of a real product design interview.

Rather than helping designers memorise answers, DesignMock AI was created to help them build confidence through deliberate practice and meaningful feedback.

Design Principles

Practice should feel realistic.

Sessions mirror the pacing and uncertainty of real interviews so users can rehearse how they think aloud, not just what they say.

Feedback should be actionable.

Every critique is anchored to communication behaviours and interview expectations, so improvement points can be applied in the very next session.

Progress should be measurable.

The product tracks performance across sessions to reveal trends in clarity, storytelling and strategic depth over time.

Every session should encourage improvement.

Recommendations are framed as next steps, helping users convert feedback into focused practice rather than passive score watching.

Designing the Experience

A realistic interview experience is more than asking questions. It requires uncertainty, follow-up conversations, structured evaluation and meaningful reflection. Every interaction inside DesignMock AI was shaped to recreate those moments while staying approachable for designers at different stages of their careers. The flow begins by reducing ambiguity: users understand what they will practise, why it matters and how progress will be measured. From there, each step builds deliberate focus — choosing context before starting, answering in a conversational rhythm, receiving critique mapped to interview expectations, then returning for another round with clearer intent. The product was designed to protect cognitive energy during high-pressure practice. Rather than overwhelming users with options, it narrows each moment to the most meaningful next decision. This creates an experience that feels steady and realistic, helping designers build confidence through repetition rather than performative perfection.

Experience Walkthrough

A single promise builds clarity quickly.

The landing experience prioritises one clear promise so users can immediately understand the value of the product without scanning through competing feature claims. This decision reduces cognitive load at the most fragile point of the journey: the first impression.

By limiting early choices and using a straightforward navigation hierarchy, the experience creates confidence and trust. Users can identify what happens next, how practice works and where to begin, which lowers friction and encourages action.

Context before questions creates realism.

Before the interview begins, users choose role, seniority and interview type. This framing step ensures prompts are not generic. It mirrors how real interviews are contextual, with expectations shaped by level, responsibilities and decision scope.

Customised setup also improves preparation quality. Designers practise articulating decisions that are relevant to the conversations they are likely to face, making each session feel less like a simulation and more like targeted rehearsal.

The interview interaction was designed to feel conversational rather than transactional. Instead of rigid form-filling, the flow encourages thoughtful responses, then adapts with follow-up prompts that keep the discussion moving naturally.

This pacing reduces intimidation and helps users stay focused on reasoning under pressure. By keeping the interface quiet and direct, attention stays on thinking quality, narrative structure and confidence in communication.

Why a conversation instead of a questionnaire?

Questionnaires test memory. Conversations reveal thinking. Recruiters rarely evaluate whether a candidate can recite definitions; they evaluate how that candidate reasons through ambiguity, explains trade-offs and connects outcomes to decisions. DesignMock AI is intentionally structured around dialogue because dialogue naturally exposes communication patterns, product judgment and structured problem solving in ways fixed-answer formats cannot.

Designing Feedback

Communication

Communication exists as a dedicated category because strong ideas lose value when explained without clarity. This score reinforces that articulation is part of design competence, not separate from it.

Storytelling

Storytelling evaluates whether candidates can connect problem framing, design decisions and outcomes into a coherent narrative. This mirrors how interviewers assess strategic maturity, not just isolated deliverables.

Product Thinking

Product Thinking encourages designers to relate interface decisions to user outcomes and business priorities. It pushes beyond aesthetic preference toward decision-making grounded in impact.

Metrics

Metrics reinforces accountability. Rather than vague claims, candidates are nudged to discuss measurable change, evidence and the value of their work in concrete terms.

Recommendation

The recommendation summarises readiness while identifying where practice should focus next. It converts feedback into direction, helping users move from evaluation to action.

Deliberate practice is not a single event. It is a cycle. Designers improve when they repeat meaningful sessions, receive specific feedback, reflect honestly and apply lessons in the next attempt. That sequence builds self-awareness over time: users begin noticing where they become vague, where stories lose structure and where outcomes are not clearly expressed.

Repetition also changes confidence. Instead of treating interviews as unpredictable performance moments, users begin to recognise familiar patterns and respond with more control. Each iteration reduces anxiety because progress becomes visible and directional. The goal is not perfection after one interview. The goal is steady growth across multiple sessions, where each conversation sharpens reasoning, communication and clarity under pressure.

Micro Interactions

Interaction design was treated as quiet support. Transitions are smooth but restrained, feedback appears quickly, and motion avoids drawing attention away from the conversation itself. The interface stays readable and responsive so users can focus on answers, not UI mechanics.

Every micro interaction was designed to reduce friction: minimal distractions, immediate response cues and clear state changes. The product should feel calm under pressure, helping concentration rather than competing for it.

Key Design Decisions

Making AI feel conversational.

The flow prioritises dialogue pacing and follow-up continuity so users practise thinking through discussion, not isolated prompts.

Reducing interview anxiety.

Clear progression, predictable structure and focused tasks lower uncertainty and help users remain composed during practice.

Encouraging measurable storytelling.

Prompts and scoring reinforce outcome-oriented narratives that connect decisions to impact, evidence and business context.

Providing actionable feedback.

Insights are framed as next steps, helping users translate critique into immediate, repeatable behavioural improvements.

Creating a repeatable learning loop.

Interview, feedback and reflection are intentionally connected so progress compounds over multiple sessions.

Designing for confidence rather than perfection.

The system rewards clarity and growth, helping users build reliable performance habits instead of chasing flawless answers.

“The goal wasn’t to simulate an interviewer perfectly. It was to create an environment where designers could think more clearly, communicate more confidently and improve with every conversation.”
